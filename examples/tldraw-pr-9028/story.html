    Steal a shape's look: hover it, press shift+Q, draw

    A format painter for the canvas — the styles of the shape under your cursor become the styles of the next shape you create.

    Act 1 · The picture

    You spend a minute getting one shape exactly right — violet, cross-hatch fill, dashed stroke, the good font. Then you want a second shape that matches. Until now that meant rebuilding the look by hand: reopen the style panel, re-pick each property, dropdown by dropdown, hoping you remember them all.

    Now: hover the shape whose look you like, press shift+Q, and draw. The new shape is born wearing the same outfit. One key, no menus — the canvas equivalent of a format painter.

    Act 2 · The journey

    shift+QThe eyedropper moment

    When you press the shortcut, tldraw asks “what shape is under the cursor right now?” — honoring edges over fills, ignoring text labels, respecting locked shapes only if your editor allows selecting them, and widening the hit area as you zoom out. If nothing is there, nothing happens. It also refuses to fire unless the editor is idle — hovering mid-drag or while a selection box is open does nothing, so the key can't ambush you in the middle of a gesture.

    Then the copy: every style the hovered shape's type knows — color, fill, dash, size, font… — is written into the editor's “next shape” memory. With one deliberate exception: the shape's kind. Stealing a star's look will not turn your next rectangle into a star.

    Plain words · The “next shape” memory

    tldraw always remembers the styles your next shape will wear — that's why a new rectangle comes out in the color you last picked. It's per-user mood, not part of the drawing itself. This feature simply writes a hovered shape's styles into that memory — like dipping your brush in someone else's paint.

    Why it matters

    A global single-chord shortcut that fires at the wrong moment is how features get hated.

    The action refuses to run unless the editor's state path ends in .idle (actions.tsx:1864-1865) — typing in a label, dragging, or brushing are not idle states, and a test pins the brushing case as a no-op (copyHoveredStyles.test.tsx:90-111). Requiring shift also keeps it away from plain typing.

    Why it matters

    Press shift+Q and… nothing visible happens until you draw the next shape. Silent success is indistinguishable from silent failure (wrong hover target, non-idle state).

    What I found

    The diff adds no feedback — no toast, no cursor change, no style-panel flash. The copy is also deliberately not undoable: the code comment (actions.tsx:1867-1868) explains next-shape styles are per-user instance state, not document state — low stakes, since it only affects shapes you haven't drawn yet.

    Try the feature once; if the silence bothers you, ask the author whether the style panel should briefly highlight on a successful copy.

    checked — looks rightWhy doesn't copying a star make my next shape a star?

    What I found

    Deliberate: the loop skips GeoShapeGeoStyle — the property that says which geometric shape this is (actions.tsx:1872). Look is copied; identity is not. Worth knowing: no test pins this exception, so a future refactor could silently drop it.

    Optionally ask for a one-line test asserting the geo kind survives a copy.

    ask the authorShould locked shapes be style donors?

    What I found

    The hover hit-test honors locked shapes only when the editor option selectLockedShapes is on (actions.tsx:1860) — by default, hovering a locked shape copies nothing. Copying a style doesn't modify the locked shape, so the stricter behavior may be borrowed caution rather than intent.

    One question: is locking meant to protect a shape's geometry, or also to keep its look from being copied?
